## GHG Protocol for SMBs

The GHG Protocol Corporate Standard is the methodology Aclymate is built on. It organizes emissions into three scopes:

**Scope 1 — Direct emissions**
Emissions from sources your company owns or controls: combustion in boilers and furnaces, company-owned vehicles, refrigerant leaks, on-site processes. If your company burns fuel or runs equipment, that's Scope 1.

**Scope 2 — Purchased energy**
Emissions from electricity, steam, heat, or cooling you buy from a utility. The emissions happen at the power plant, not at your location — but you're responsible for them because you created the demand. Reported two ways:
- *Location-based*: uses the average grid emissions factor for your region
- *Market-based*: uses the actual emissions factor of your energy supplier or any RECs you've purchased. If you buy renewable energy certificates, market-based can be lower than location-based.

**Scope 3 — Everything else**
Indirect emissions in your value chain. For most SMBs, this is the largest scope — often 70–90% of total footprint. The GHG Protocol defines 15 Scope 3 categories, but the ones that matter most for SMBs:
- Category 1: Purchased goods and services (what your vendors emit to serve you)
- Category 6: Business travel (flights, hotels, rental cars)
- Category 7: Employee commuting
- Category 11: Use of sold products (if you make something that generates emissions when used)

Most SMBs start by measuring Scope 1 and 2, then add Scope 3 over time as data becomes available.

## Industry Footprint Profiles

Rough annual footprint ranges for a company of ~20–50 employees. Actual figures vary significantly by location, facility type, and operations.

**Professional services** (law firms, consultancies, agencies, 20 employees)
- Range: 15–40 tCO2e/year
- Dominant sources: Business travel (Scope 3 Cat. 6), employee commuting (Scope 3 Cat. 7), office electricity (Scope 2)
- Scope 3 is typically 75%+ of total footprint
- Reduction levers: Remote work policy, travel substitution with video, green electricity tariff

**Restaurants and food service** (50 employees, full-service)
- Range: 80–250 tCO2e/year
- Dominant sources: Natural gas for cooking (Scope 1), refrigerant leaks (Scope 1), food purchasing (Scope 3 Cat. 1 — beef and dairy are the biggest drivers)
- Food sourcing can account for 40–60% of total footprint
- Reduction levers: Menu composition (less beef), refrigerant management, efficient equipment

**Retail** (storefront, 20 employees)
- Range: 30–100 tCO2e/year
- Dominant sources: Electricity for lighting and HVAC (Scope 2), purchased merchandise (Scope 3 Cat. 1)
- Scope 3 from products often dwarfs in-store emissions
- Reduction levers: LED lighting, renewable electricity, supplier engagement

**Technology / SaaS** (20 employees, no physical product)
- Range: 8–25 tCO2e/year
- Dominant sources: Employee commuting (Scope 3 Cat. 7), business travel (Scope 3 Cat. 6), cloud infrastructure (Scope 3 Cat. 1 — AWS/GCP/Azure have published emission factors)
- Often the lowest-footprint SMB category per employee
- Reduction levers: Remote-first policy, green cloud providers, travel policy

**Light manufacturing** (50 employees)
- Range: 150–600 tCO2e/year
- Dominant sources: Process energy (Scope 1 + 2), purchased raw materials (Scope 3 Cat. 1)
- Scope 1 and 2 dominate unlike most service businesses
- Reduction levers: Process efficiency, electrification, renewable energy procurement

**Healthcare / medical practices** (20 employees)
- Range: 25–70 tCO2e/year
- Dominant sources: Electricity (Scope 2), medical supplies (Scope 3 Cat. 1), anesthetic gases where applicable (Scope 1)
- Reduction levers: Energy efficiency, supply chain engagement, waste reduction

**Construction and trades** (20 employees)
- Range: 40–150 tCO2e/year
- Dominant sources: Diesel equipment and vehicles (Scope 1), purchased materials like concrete and steel (Scope 3 Cat. 1)
- Project-based businesses should consider project-level reporting
- Reduction levers: Equipment electrification, material substitution (low-carbon concrete)

## Key Concepts and Vocabulary

**Emission factors**
A number that converts an activity into a CO2 equivalent. Examples: 0.21 kg CO2e per kWh of US average electricity; 0.255 kg CO2e per mile for a passenger car; 27 kg CO2e per kg of beef. Published by EPA, IPCC, DEFRA, and grid operators. Factors are updated periodically — always note the vintage year.

**CO2e (carbon dioxide equivalent)**
A single unit that accounts for all greenhouse gases weighted by their warming potential. Methane is 25× more warming than CO2 over 100 years; nitrous oxide is 298×. CO2e lets you add them all up into one number.

**Carbon neutral vs. net zero**
These are not the same thing.
- *Carbon neutral* typically means offsetting your emissions for a specific year — you buy enough offsets to balance what you emitted. It says nothing about reducing.
- *Net zero* means reaching a state where you've reduced emissions as far as scientifically possible and offset only the residual. SBTi defines this as a 90%+ reduction from a baseline before any offsets.
- *Carbon negative / climate positive* means removing more from the atmosphere than you emit.

**Market-based vs. location-based (Scope 2)**
Companies report both under the GHG Protocol. Location-based uses the regional grid average. Market-based reflects actual contractual arrangements — RECs, PPAs, or supplier-specific rates. If a company buys wind energy credits, their market-based Scope 2 can be near zero even if the location-based figure is high.

**Audit-ready data**
A GHG report is audit-ready when every data point has a traceable source: utility bills for Scope 2, mileage logs for vehicle emissions, expense reports for travel. "Estimate-grade" data (using spend × emission factors) is fine for a first baseline but not sufficient for third-party verification.

**Baseline year**
The reference year against which all future reductions are measured. Most companies pick their first year of complete data. The GHG Protocol allows for baseline recalculation if the business changes significantly (acquisitions, divestitures).

## What Good Looks Like

A realistic first-year carbon accounting journey for an SMB:

**Months 1–2: Baseline estimate**
Collect what's available — utility bills, travel expense reports, fleet fuel records. Fill gaps with spend-based estimates (dollars spent × emission factor per dollar). Scope 1 and 2 are usually complete within weeks. Scope 3 takes longer.

**Months 3–6: Refine key sources**
Identify the 2–3 emission sources that dominate (often business travel and/or purchased goods). Invest in better data for those. The 80/20 rule applies — a small number of categories drive most of the footprint.

**Months 6–12: Set a target and report**
Publish an internal emissions report. Set a reduction target — even a simple "reduce Scope 1+2 by 30% by 2028" is meaningful. Consider a voluntary disclosure (CDP, B Corp, customer-facing statement).

**Year 2+: Systematic reduction**
Work reduction initiatives into budget cycles. Engage top vendors on Scope 3 data. Consider science-based target validation if you want external credibility.
